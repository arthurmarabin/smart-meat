01 · Problem

The retail meat gap is widening.

Consumers feel the price pressure, while most subscription competitors sell convenience as a premium experience instead of a smarter way to buy.

01

Meat prices are rising

Weekly grocery spend keeps climbing, making meat an obvious target for household savings.

02

Retail markup is high

The customer pays for fragmentation, display, waste, and layers of convenience.

03

Competitors are premium

Most subscription brands position around curation, not affordability.

04

Consumers need alternatives

A practical purchase model can become the value proposition.

02 · Opportunity

Cheaper supply plus predictable demand.

The business can win by simplifying the offer, reducing waste, and matching supplier purchasing with subscriber demand.

A

Direct supplier access

Start with one reliable supply path and negotiate around volume predictability.

B

Lower cost per pound

Pass part of the savings to customers while preserving enough contribution margin.

C

Subscription model

Recurring demand helps plan purchasing, packing, routes, and cash flow.

D

Predictable demand

Order windows replace speculation with committed purchases.

E

Lean operation

One cut, one region, tight cadence, and manual operations before scale.

F

Clear customer logic

Customers save because they buy the right format, not because quality is lower.

06 · Lean mode

Validate before building the machine.

The first version should prove demand, price sensitivity, supplier reliability, and delivery cadence.

Lean

Sem estoque alto

Buy against subscriber commitment wherever possible.

Lean

Sem estrutura grande

Temporary processes are acceptable while demand is being tested.

MVP

App MVP via Vibe Coding

Use simple technology to make the model tangible.

Focus

Foco em validação

Measure willingness to subscribe, repeat, and refer.

Scope

1 corte, 1 região

Constrain variation until operations are stable.

Pilot

30–50 clientes

Enough to expose real issues without excessive complexity.

07 · Decision

Who is the first customer?

Multiple choices allowed. The sharper the pilot audience, the easier it is to choose product, message, route, and price.

08 · Decision

Where should the first route run?

Pick one launch area to keep delivery, feedback, and word-of-mouth concentrated.

09 · Decision

Which cut should anchor the pilot?

The first cut should balance supplier access, customer recognition, margin, and cooking confidence.

10 · Decision

How much technology is enough?

Choose the smallest tech surface that can collect payment, explain the product, and support repeat orders.

11 · Decision

How many pilot subscribers?

The goal is a pilot large enough to reveal economics and operational friction without pretending to be scale.
